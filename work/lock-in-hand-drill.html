Lock-In Hand Drill

Year
2024
Role
Designer / Course Project (INDN 301)
Tools
Fusion 360, FDM 3D Printing (PLA, TPU)

Overview

Lock-In is a concept redesign of the everyday hand drill, built around a persona of an experienced handyman developing arthritis. Most modern drills offer little in the way of ergonomic support beyond the trigger grip — they assume one strong, steady hand and leave the other to fend for itself. Lock-In rethinks that assumption, giving both hands a stable, comfortable place to work from.

Design brief

The persona driving this project: an experienced handyman whose hands are starting to fight him. Off-the-shelf drills don't offer ergonomic solutions for the support hand, only the trigger hand. Digging through my grandfather's shed turned up several older drills that quietly solved this — wider, softer grips usable with either hand, and in some cases a built-in bubble level for keeping a hole straight without guesswork. Lock-In's brief came directly out of that observation: build a drill that's comfortable for both hands and gives the user leveling reference at multiple angles.

Process

Because a fully custom drill mechanism was out of scope for a semester project, I designed Lock-In to be compatible with internal parts from a Harbor Freight Warrior drill, letting the concept focus on the ergonomics and housing rather than reinventing the motor and gearing. Early 3D printing was rough going, but it let me iterate fast on the actual grip geometry.

To find the right feel, I designed interchangeable grip plates so I could test different shapes, textures, and materials head-to-head:

  • Full CAD model in Fusion 360, built around the donor drill's internal components
  • Swappable grip plates for rapid material and texture comparisons
  • Printed in both PLA and TPU to compare rigid vs. soft-touch grip feel
  • Integrated leveling references informed by the vintage drills that inspired the brief

The winner wasn't obvious going in — yellow PLA ended up looking the best on the shelf, but white TPU felt the best in hand, which says a lot about the gap between how a product photographs and how it actually performs.

Outcome

The final deliverable was a complete CAD package and a printed, hand-assembled prototype built around real drill internals — proof that the ergonomic concept could live inside a working tool, not just a rendering. The project pushed me to design around real hardware constraints instead of a blank sheet, and to treat "comfortable to hold" as a spec you can actually test, not just a nice sentence in a brief.

Tools used: Fusion 360 for CAD and grip iteration, FDM printing (PLA and TPU) for prototyping, Harbor Freight Warrior drill internals for the working mechanism.
